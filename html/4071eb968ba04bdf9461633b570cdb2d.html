This tells us that 75% of the N2 that is fed into the reactor is going unused (i.e. wasted)!  As discussed in Module 7, a major goal of chemical process engineers is to “beat equilibrium”.  We can do this by recycling the unused reactants back into the reactor to achieve higher overall conversion of N2 and H2.

 

Consider: Ammonia Synthesis With Recycle.  In order to recycle unused reactants, we need to add a separator to separate the N2 and H2 coming out of the reactor.

 

Define: single-pass conversion and overall conversion

It is useful to differentiate between two conversions: the single-pass conversion for a single pass through the reactor and the overall conversion for the overall process.  These are illustrated in the above PFD.

\[ single-pass\ conversion=\ \frac{moles\ reactant\ \bf{reacted\ in\ reactor}}{moles\ reactant\ \bf{fed\ to\ reactor}} \]

\[ overall\ conversion=\ \frac{moles\ reactant\ \bf{reacted\ in\ process}}{moles\ reactant\ \bf{fed\ to\ process}} \]

Let’s again assume we have a stoichiometric feed of N2 and H2 and the same single-pass conversion (0.25) through the reactor.   Also assume that we have a perfect separator such that our recycle stream contains only N2 and H2 and our product stream contains only NH3. 

  • For this idealized process, what is our overall conversion assuming that we have the same productivity as our process without recycle?  We find that we can achieve an overall conversion of 1!  (Real processes can come close to this with overall conversions of 0.98-0.99.)   We can achieve the same productivity as the process without recycle while getting complete conversion of our stoichiometric feed.
